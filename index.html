To me AI is like a two-way street – in one direction there is the approach to creating technology that aims to meet an “intelligence threshold”. This is where we use our definition of intelligence David Weschler: “To judge well, to comprehend well, to reason well”, Philip E Vernon “the effective all-round cognitive abilities to comprehend, to grasp relations and reason” as a measure with which to judge and improve artificial intelligence. We are excellent at recognising intelligent behaviours in both ourselves and other animals, but we are still working on how to translate those behaviours to computers. It probably doesn’t help that our understanding of our own intelligence is incomplete – subject to cognitive biases, enduring mysteries within consciousness, sleep and creativity. In this category is robotics, machine learning and Skynet.

In the other direction, is where we use technology to improve and model how our brain works. This includes fMRIs, eye-tracking, cognitive modelling and adaptive learning environments. This, for me, is where things get really exciting – our brains are excellent at pattern matching or drawing inferences, but we start to struggle when it comes to large datasets, and we have used computers to fill this gap. Through data science we can crunch through massive amounts of data, can view brain activity more effectively than ever before and create ever more complex models of our own operating system.

And it’s a perfect feedback loop, right? As we learn more about our own brains and intelligence, we can feed that back into making technology more effective and that then empowers to delve even deeper into the realm of neuroscience. It’s an exciting time to be interested in the brain!

And really, why wouldn’t you be interested in the brain? My ambitions of being a ballerina or an astronaut or an Olympic gymnast have sadly fallen by the wayside, so instead I make a living using my brain. You can use a computer without knowing how it works, you can drive a car without knowing auto-mechanics, but if something goes wrong it helps, right? Having an inkling of what’s going on inside that black box that sits on top of your shoulders could be helpful in any area of your life from relationships, to illnesses to losing weight, but we are going to look at it from the perspective of a problem solver.

And, more specifically, a problem solver who is under a level of stress. So, let’s have a look at you – you, on a really a bad day….

Tired

Why might your brain be tired?

  • You need to sleep
  • You need to take a break
  • You need to recharge your batteries

These might all sound fairly similar, but there is a great deal of research underpinning the benefits that these confer. We all know we need to sleep – but do you know why? Do you know why we struggle so much when we have slept poorly? We all know that feeling when we walk away from a problem only to solve it hours later, but why does this happen?
