Tired

Why might your brain be tired?

  • You need to sleep
  • You need to take a break
  • You need to recharge your batteries

We all know we need to sleep – but do you know why? Do you know why we struggle so much when we have slept poorly? We all know that feeling when we walk away from a problem only to solve it hours later, but why does this happen?

Let’s talk about what is happening in the brain when you are tired.

Sleep deprived

Ever tried to teach a tired child anything?

Push and pull between amygdala (anger, irritability, anxiety, fear) and neocortex (consciousness, attention, perception, reasoning)

Mitochondria are tiny structure that fuel the body by producing molecules called adenosine triphosphate or ATP...

+ raised blood pressure + harder to control emotions

SW sleep preserves our memories by recapitulating what we have learned during the day. REM incorporates these memories into what we already know.

Tetris

David Eagleman references studies that attempted to teach retrograde amnesiacs how to play tetris.

Not surprising - what's good for body is good for mind. + exercise promotes growth hormones, protein rich foods aid neuroplasticity

The wandering mind

Your mind is like a town - when focused everyone is in the town square, but when wandering activity is distributed. Default mode network.

Benefits of mindfulness are well documented. + shrunken amygdala + enlarged prefrontal cortex. During mind wandering, subjects report less happy than not.

Ever daydreamed through a green light or asked someone for the time only to have zoned out by the time they tell you the answer? So why do we do it?

Unusual Use Tasks

Baird & Smallwood conducted a study - tasks without a working memory load elicit more mind wandering than tasks with a working memory load... [Unusual Use Tasks] Incubation++

  • attentional cycling - honing mental juggling skills
  • dishabituation - refreshed attention, brain responds to change
  • relief from boredom - ability to persevere with tedious but necessary tasks

Ask for help

Your needs are not obvious. Can try hovering and hoping, but not super effective

Heidi Grant in her TED Talk outlines...

  • be realistic

  • follow up after

The flawed story

Sometimes flawed stories delight us - magic! But not usually in programming.

Why is story so important?

In Yuval Noah Harari's book - Sapiens - he talks about an individual being able to "keep up" with ~150 other people, and posits that we are only able to successfully cooperate as a species through the means of shared fiction

We are built for story, it is in everything we do, think and want. A healthy mind is the "central protagonist" in their own hero epic

Rubber ducks

Play to your brain's strengths:

  • Speak/write your problem out, because by sequencing it you are removing it from your RAM and ordering it. Can see your own flaws. Rubber duck debugging!
  • Also, by speaking you are hearing and by writing you are seeing. Brain is like a city.

The 3 Bs

Give your brain more to work with: David Eagleman & Anthony Brandt in their book The Runaway Species talk about the 3 B's of creativity...

  • bending: adapt what is already out there
  • breaking: reductionism - boil it down to its component parts
  • blending: combine sources or find related/similar problem spaces
